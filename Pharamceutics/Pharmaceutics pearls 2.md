- **Deliquescence** occurs when a substance's solubility is so high that it absorbs enough atmospheric water to dissolve completely and form a liquid solution, representing the extreme end of hygroscopicity. While hygroscopicity refers to the general ability to attract water molecules, deliquescence involves a bulk phase change into a solution, unlike water adsorption which is strictly a surface phenomenon.
- **Chlorobutanol** serves as a classic antimicrobial preservative in pharmaceutical solutions, particularly in ophthalmic and parenteral formulations at concentrations around 0.5%, to prevent microbial growth. Although it possesses a camphor-like odor, its functional role is preservation rather than flavoring, and it is distinct from antioxidants like ascorbic acid.
- In **flocculated pharmaceutical suspensions**, particles form loose aggregates called "flocs" that sediment rapidly to form a large, porous mass, leaving a clear supernatant. Because of this porous structure, the sediment does not "cake" and is easily resuspended, whereas deflocculated systems settle slowly into a hard, irreversible cake with a turbid supernatant.
- **Carboxymethyl Cellulose (CMC)** is a widely used hydrophilic polymer that acts as a suspending agent by increasing the viscosity of the liquid vehicle to slow down particle sedimentation. It is more standard and versatile for oral liquids than insoluble polymers like ethyl cellulose or cellulose acetate phthalate, which are typically reserved for tablet coatings.
- **Polyvalent metal salts of fatty acids**, such as calcium or magnesium soaps, are lipophilic and typically favor the formation of **Water-in-Oil (w/o) emulsions**, whereas monovalent salts like sodium or potassium soaps are hydrophilic and create Oil-in-Water (o/w) emulsions.
- To achieve a specific **Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance (HLB)** of 12 using surfactant A (HLB 10) and surfactant B (HLB 16), a 2:1 ratio is required, as determined by the equation $12 = 10x + 16(1-x)$. This HLB system ranks surfactants from 1 to 20, where lower values indicate lipophilic properties (w/o emulsifiers) and higher values indicate hydrophilic properties (o/w emulsifiers).
- **Coalescence** in pharmaceutical emulsions is the irreversible fusion of dispersed phase droplets caused by the breaking of the protective interfacial film, which eventually leads to complete phase separation. This is distinct from creaming, which is a reversible density-driven movement, or flocculation, where droplets form loose clusters but remain individual.
- **Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) 4000** is a solid, waxy polymer at room temperature that is ideal for melt granulation, as it melts at relatively low temperatures (50–80°C) to act as a binder. Lower molecular weight versions like PEG 200 or 400 are liquids used primarily as solvents or plasticizers.
- A **Fluidized Bed Dryer (FBD)** is a versatile device that can be equipped with spray nozzles to perform "one-pot" wet granulation and drying within the same unit. This differs from spray dryers, which produce powders directly from liquid feeds, or freeze dryers, which are used for lyophilizing heat-sensitive materials.
- **Segregation**, also known as demixing, refers to the separation of powder components due to differences in particle size and density during flow or vibration, which can compromise the content uniformity of a pharmaceutical blend.
- **Lozenges (troches)** are formulated with bases like **Mannitol** for a cooling sensation or **Gelatin** for a gummy texture, alongside lubricants like **Sodium Stearyl Fumarate** to ensure they erode slowly in the mouth. Unlike standard tablets, they exclude disintegrants like starch or sodium starch glycolate to prevent them from breaking apart prematurely in saliva.
- **Starch** is a high-yield **disintegrant** in tablet formulations that works through capillary action (wicking) and swelling to break the tablet into fragments upon contact with water, thereby increasing the surface area for dissolution.
- **Viscoelastic excipients** exhibit deformation that is **strain rate sensitive**, meaning their behavior depends on how fast the compression force is applied. If the dwell time during tableting is too short, these materials may fail to deform properly, leading to common defects like capping or lamination.
- In an **insoluble polymer matrix slow-release system**, the drug is embedded in a rigid hydrophobic structure where release is governed by the drug dissolving in GI fluid and diffusing out through a network of tortuous (winding) pores. The release rate in these systems is specifically affected by the porosity and tortuosity of the matrix.
- **Hydrophilic matrices**, often composed of HPMC, allow for a tunable drug release pattern that can be adjusted to follow either zero-order (constant release) or first-order (diffusion-controlled) kinetics depending on the polymer concentration and drug solubility.
- **Acrylate polymers (Eudragit®)** are uniquely versatile materials that can be used for both enteric and non-enteric coatings because their side chains can be modified to make them gastric-soluble, enteric, or insoluble for sustained release.
- **Cellulose Acetate Phthalate (CAP)** is utilized during the **sealing stage** of the sugar-coating process to provide a waterproof barrier that protects the tablet core from the moisture contained in subsequent syrup layers.
- An **Auger filling machine** requires the hard gelatin capsule shell to be completely filled to ensure dosage uniformity, whereas tamping or dosator machines create a powder "plug" that allows for partial fills independent of the capsule's total volume.
- **Preservatives** are permitted in **Small Volume Parenterals (SVP)**, especially in multi-dose vials, but are strictly forbidden in Large Volume Parenterals (LVP), intraspinal, and intraocular injections due to potential toxicity or irritation.
- The **absorption process** is observed in **subcutaneous** and intramuscular routes where the drug must diffuse through tissue to enter systemic circulation, whereas intrathecal or intraocular routes are considered local administrations.
- To maximize the deposition of aerosol particles in the lower airways, the ideal breathing pattern is a **slow, deep breath followed by breath-holding**, which minimizes turbulence and allows time for the particles to settle via gravity.
- The **carrier** in dry powder inhalers (DPIs), typically **Lactose**, is used to improve the flowability of tiny, cohesive drug particles; during inhalation, turbulence de-aggregates the drug from the carrier, allowing the medicine to reach the lungs while the carrier impacts the throat.
- **Viscosity-increasing agents** like methylcellulose are the most effective strategy for increasing the residence time of drugs in the nose by slowing down the natural mucociliary clearance of the nasal mucosa.
- **Mucoadhesive materials** used in nasal products, such as **Chitosan, Pectin, and Hydroxypropyl Cellulose**, stabilize the formulation by forming hydrogen bonds or electrostatic interactions with nasal mucin.
- Adding **surfactants** to ophthalmic formulations can cause incompatibilities with **microbial preservatives** like Benzalkonium Chloride, potentially leading to complexation or micellar entrapment that leaves the product unprotected.
- **Borate buffers** are frequently used in ophthalmic drops because they are non-irritating and do not catalyze the **hydrolysis of amide or ester bonds** found in local anesthetics or alkaloids, unlike phosphate buffers which can cause corneal deposits.
- **Polyethylene** is the most suitable material for the **backing layer** of skin patches because it is occlusive, flexible, and chemically inert, preventing the loss of moisture or drug from the system during long-term use.
- For dry, thick, or scaly skin lesions, an **ointment** is the most suitable topical product because its hydrocarbon base is occlusive, trapping moisture to hydrate and soften the stratum corneum.
- **Aerosil (Colloidal Silicon Dioxide)** acts as a viscosity-increasing agent in rectal suppositories to prevent drug particles from sedimenting while the molten base cools, ensuring a uniform distribution of the active ingredient.
- A **pessary** is a pharmaceutical term for a solid dosage form intended for vaginal administration, which can take the form of either a molded suppository or a compressed vaginal tablet.
- **Spoilage** of a pharmaceutical product is defined by the entry and growth of microbes that result in perceptible consequences, such as gas production, pH shifts, or the chemical breakdown of ingredients.
- **Gram-negative bacteria** like Pseudomonas are the microorganisms least likely to be transferred from a dry environment to a pharmaceutical product because they require high moisture levels to survive, unlike more resilient fungi or spore-forming bacteria.
- **Lyophilization (freeze-drying)** is a process where temperature and pressure act as the driving force for the sublimation of water from a solid to a gas phase, distinguishing it from concentration-driven passive diffusion.
- **Weak bases** are typically avoided as buffers not because of low capacity, but because their pH is highly temperature-dependent, they depend on the ion product of water ($K_w$), and they often exhibit chemical instability or unpleasant tastes.
- In **solvent extraction**, efficiency is maximized by performing multiple extractions with small volumes of secondary solvent rather than a single extraction with a large volume, as the process is governed by the distribution coefficient.
- **Steady-state diffusion** occurs when the concentration of the diffusing substance remains constant on both sides of a membrane over time, indicating that the rate of drug entry into the membrane equals the rate of exit.
- **Sink conditions** in tablet dissolution testing are established when the concentration of the drug in the medium ($C$) is kept below 10% of its maximum solubility ($C_s$), ensuring that the dissolution rate is not limited by molecular crowding.
- According to the **Arrhenius equation**, $k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}$, the reaction rate constant ($k$) **increases logarithmically** (or exponentially) as the temperature increases.
- While the **Cone and Plate viscometer** offers advantages like small sample volumes and the absence of plug flow, it still requires precise **temperature adjustment** during measurement, as temperature is the most critical factor affecting viscosity.
- The **Zeta potential** is the potential difference measured at the **shear plane** of a moving colloidal particle; it serves as the primary predictor of colloidal stability, where a high zeta potential indicates strong inter-particle repulsion.
- A **contact angle of 180 degrees** indicates that water has zero wetting on a surface, identifying the material as completely lipophilic or hydrophobic.
- The **Coulter Counter** method determines particle size by measuring **changes in electrical resistance** as particles pass through a small orifice, creating a resistance spike proportional to their volume.
- For drugs with a **high hepatic extraction ratio**, hepatic clearance is significantly impacted by **hepatic blood flow**, as the liver is so efficient at removing the drug that the rate-limiting step is the speed of delivery to the organ.
- The appearance of a **"double peak"** in a plasma concentration curve can be caused by enterohepatic circulation or irregular gastric emptying, but it is **not** typically associated with **lymphatic absorption**, which is a slow and continuous process.
- In **non-linear Michaelis-Menten kinetics**, when the plasma concentration ($C_p$) equals the Michaelis constant ($K_m$), the rate of the process is exactly half of the maximum velocity ($V_{max}/2$).
- **P-glycoprotein (P-gp)** is an ATP-dependent efflux pump that utilizes **active transport** to pump drugs out of cells, thereby decreasing intestinal absorption and intracellular penetration in various tissues.
