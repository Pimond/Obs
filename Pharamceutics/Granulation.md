Granulation is the process of particle enlargement where small powder particles are gathered into larger, permanent aggregates called **granules**. This is done to improve flowability, prevent segregation of ingredients, and enhance the compression characteristics required for tablet or capsule manufacturing.

## 1. Wet Granulation

The most widely used technique. It involves "gluing" particles together using a liquid binder (solvent + adhesive).

- **The Process:*
	1. **Dry Blending:** Mixing the Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) with diluents.
    2. **Wetting:** Adding the binder solution to form a "damp mass."
    3. **Screening:** Passing the damp mass through a mesh to form moist granules.
    4. **Drying:** Removing the solvent (usually via heat).
    5. **Dry Screening/Milling:** Sizing the dried granules for uniformity.
- **Instruments:** 
	* **High-Shear Granulators:** Uses a high-speed impeller and chopper for rapid granulation.
    - **Fluid Bed Granulators:** Suspends powder in air while spraying binder; performs mixing, granulation, and drying in one unit.
- **Wet Binders:** **PVP (Povidone)**, **HPMC**, Starch Paste, or Sucrose.
## 2. Dry Granulation

Used for materials that are sensitive to moisture or heat. It relies on high mechanical pressure to create bonds.

- **The Process:** 
	1. **Compaction:** Squeezing dry powder into "slugs" or ribbons.
    2. **Milling/Screening:** Breaking the compacted mass into smaller, uniform granules.
- **Instruments:** 
	* **Roller Compactor:** Forces powder between two counter-rotating rollers to form a ribbon.
    - **Slugger:** A heavy-duty tablet press used to make large, crude tablets ("slugs").
- **Dry Binders:** **Microcrystalline Cellulose (MCC)**, Polyethylene Glycol (PEG), or Stearic Acid.
    
## 3. Specialized Techniques

### Spray Drying

A one-step process that converts a liquid (solution or suspension) into a dry powder
- **How it works:** The liquid is atomized into a hot air chamber. The solvent evaporates instantly, leaving behind spherical, free-flowing granules.
- **Best for:** Highly uniform particles and heat-sensitive materials.
### Melt Granulation

A "water-free" wet granulation alternative.

- **How it works:** A binder with a low melting point (50°C–80°C) is mixed with the powder. The mixture is heated until the binder melts and acts as the "liquid" to join particles. It hardens upon cooling.
- **Melt Binders:** **PEG 4000/6000**, **Stearic Acid**, or Carnauba Wax.

### Foam Granulation

Binder is added as a foam rather than a spray. This uses significantly less water, shortening drying times and preventing "over-wetting" (the dreaded "banana paste" consistency).

|**Issue**|**Likely Culprit**|**Immediate Action**|
|---|---|---|
|**Capping**|Excessive "fines" or overly dry|Add moisture/binder|
|**Sticking**|Too much moisture/tackiness|Dry longer/add lubricant|
|**Mottling**|Dye migration|Increase drying speed|
|**Weight Variation**|Poor flow/irregular sizing|Re-size granules (milling)|
